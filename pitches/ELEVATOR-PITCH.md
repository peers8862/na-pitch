## 60-Second Version

> "There's a massive gap in North American electronics manufacturing. Companies like Celestica and Jabil need orders of 10,000 units minimum. On the other end, you've got garage-level assembly with no quality systems. The middle is completely empty.
>
> Babb Works fills that gap. We do professional-grade, small-batch PCB assembly — 50 to 5,000 units — for industrial IoT, edge computing, and ruggedized field equipment. But here's what makes us different: we optimize firmware at the binary level. That means 40 to 70 percent less power consumption, deterministic timing for real-time industrial applications, and the ability to run on cheaper microcontrollers. It's a technical moat that's extremely hard to replicate.
>
> We're launching in Hamilton, Ontario — Canada's manufacturing heartland, with direct access to the U.S. market through Buffalo. Phase 1 is $510K for a 4,000 square foot facility. We hit breakeven by month 12, scale to $3.4 million by year 5, and create 60 to 90 skilled jobs.
>
> With $52 billion flowing into North American chip manufacturing and electronics reshoring accelerating, the timing is perfect. We're building the infrastructure that the reshoring wave needs."

## Key Talking Points (for follow-up questions)

**Why Hamilton?**
> "60% of Canada's steel comes from Hamilton. It's the #1 express cargo airport in Canada. We have direct cross-border access to Buffalo, and FedDev Ontario has invested $2.9 billion in the region since 2015. The manufacturing DNA is already here."

**What's your technical advantage?**
> "Most firmware is written in C or C++. We optimize at the assembly and binary level — the actual machine code. That means 40 to 70 percent less power, cycle-exact timing for industrial applications, and smaller code that runs on cheaper chips. It's a rare skill set that takes years to develop."

**Who are your customers?**
> "Industrial equipment manufacturers, agricultural technology companies, building automation firms — anyone building ruggedized electronics who needs professional quality but can't meet the minimum order quantities of the big EMS providers."

**What's the exit?**
> "Build a regional electronics manufacturing ecosystem in the Niagara Peninsula — the 'Kingsville model' applied to advanced manufacturing. Long-term, this becomes an acquisition target for strategic buyers looking for North American capacity, or grows into a multi-site network."
